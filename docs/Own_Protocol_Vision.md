# Own Protocol — Permissionless Tokenized Real-World Assets — Long-Term Product Vision | March 2026

## 1. Overview

Own Protocol enables permissionless, fully collateralized exposure to real-world assets (RWAs) onchain. Users mint composable ERC-20 eTokens (eTSLA, eGOLD, eTLT, etc.) by depositing collateral. Liquidity providers (LPs) supply collateral to public vaults managed by vault managers and earn yield through a unified spread model.

The protocol is deployed on Base (Ethereum L2) and built with Foundry.

### Core Thesis

Traditional finance gatekeeps asset exposure behind brokerage accounts, KYC, geographic restrictions, and market-hour limitations. Own Protocol removes these barriers by creating permissionless synthetic exposure backed by real collateral and managed hedging, accessible 24/7 to anyone with a wallet.

---

## 2. Protocol Participants

### Minters

Any address can mint eTokens by depositing supported collateral. Minters receive eTokens representing synthetic exposure to the underlying RWA. To close a position, minters redeem eTokens and receive collateral back, minus the applicable spread.

Minters are treated as untrusted. All minter input is adversarial.

### Liquidity Providers (LPs)

LPs deposit collateral into public vaults and receive ERC-4626 vault shares representing their proportional ownership. LPs earn yield from spread revenue generated when minters mint and redeem eTokens.

LPs can also act as their own vault manager, managing their own hedging and spread parameters without delegating to a third party.

### Vault Managers

Vault managers (formerly "curators") actively manage the offchain hedging obligations for a vault's exposure and set custom spread parameters. Their responsibilities include:

- Maintaining offchain hedges to ensure the vault remains solvent against RWA price movements
- Setting custom spreads for non-market hours and volatile conditions
- Monitoring vault health and adjusting parameters as needed

A vault can have multiple vault managers. LPs delegate to vault managers through a mutual agreement model — the LP selects a vault manager, and the vault manager accepts the delegation. This ensures both parties consent to the arrangement.

Vault managers earn a share of the spread revenue generated by the exposure they manage.

### Protocol Admin

A multisig that governs protocol-level parameters: asset whitelisting, oracle configuration, minimum spread floors, and emergency halt capabilities.

### Liquidation Bots

Untrusted external actors that monitor vault health and trigger liquidation when a vault's collateral ratio falls below the required threshold. Liquidation bots are incentivized by a reward paid from the liquidated vault.

## 3. Supported Assets

### eTokens

Each supported RWA has a corresponding eToken — a standard ERC-20 token with ERC-2612 (Permit) support:

| eToken | Underlying | Category |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| eTSLA  | Tesla stock | US Equities |
| eGOLD  | Gold (XAU) | Commodities |
| eTLT   | 20+ Year Treasury Bond ETF | Fixed Income |

eTokens are 18-decimal ERC-20 tokens. They are composable with the broader DeFi ecosystem — holdable, transferable, and usable as collateral in lending protocols.

### Collateral Types (Multi-Collateral from Launch)

Own Protocol supports multiple collateral types from day one:

| Collateral | Decimals | Yield-Bearing | Handling |
|-----------|----------|---------------|----------|
| USDC | 6 | No | Direct deposit |
| aUSDC (Aave) | 6 | Yes (rebasing) | Yield captured via ERC-4626 wrapper; rebasing balance accrues to the vault |
| ETH | 18 | No | Wrapped to WETH internally via WETHRouter |
| stETH (Lido) | 18 | Yes (exchange rate) | Wrapped to wstETH internally; yield captured via wstETH/stETH exchange rate appreciation |

### Yield-Bearing Collateral Mechanics

**aUSDC**: Aave's interest-bearing USDC is a rebasing token. When deposited as collateral, the vault wraps it in an ERC-4626-compliant adapter. The accruing yield increases the vault's total collateral value, benefiting LPs without any action required.

**stETH**: Lido's liquid staking derivative is internally wrapped to wstETH (wrapped stETH) on deposit. wstETH is a non-rebasing token whose value relative to stETH increases over time as staking rewards accumulate. The vault tracks collateral value using the wstETH/stETH exchange rate, and LPs benefit from the yield.

## 4. Mint and Redeem Lifecycle

### Minting

1. Minter selects an asset (e.g., TSLA) and a collateral type (e.g., USDC)
2. Oracle provides a signed price for the asset
3. Protocol validates the price (signature, staleness, chain ID, contract address)
4. Minter deposits collateral; protocol calculates eToken amount based on the oracle price and applicable spread
5. eTokens are minted to the minter's address
6. Collateral is locked in the vault; vault manager's hedging obligation increases

### Redeeming

1. Minter submits eTokens for redemption and specifies desired collateral type
2. Oracle provides a signed price
3. Protocol validates the price
4. Protocol burns the eTokens and calculates collateral owed, minus the applicable spread
5. Collateral is released to the minter
6. Vault manager's hedging obligation decreases

### Non-Market Hours

When traditional markets are closed, vault managers set wider spreads to compensate for the inability to adjust offchain hedges in real time. The protocol enforces a minimum spread floor at all times, but vault managers can set spreads above this floor during non-market hours or periods of elevated volatility.

## 5. Unified Spread Model

Own Protocol uses a unified spread model. There is no separate "protocol fee" and "vault manager spread" — a single spread is applied to every mint and redeem operation.

### Minimum Spread (Protocol Floor)

The protocol enforces a `minSpread` parameter — the minimum spread that any vault must charge. This floor ensures the protocol always generates revenue regardless of competitive pressure between vault managers.

The `minSpread` is set by the protocol admin and applies uniformly across all vaults.

### Custom Spreads

Vault managers set custom spreads at or above the `minSpread` floor. Typical configurations:

- **Market hours (liquid hedging available)**: Spread near the `minSpread` floor to remain competitive
- **Non-market hours**: Wider spreads (e.g., 100-300 BPS) to compensate for hedging risk
- **High volatility events**: Temporarily elevated spreads

### Revenue Split

Spread revenue from each mint/redeem operation is split three ways:

| Recipient | Share | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Protocol | Fixed portion of spread (from `minSpread` floor) | Protocol sustainability, development, security audits |
| Vault Manager | Configurable share of spread above floor | Compensation for active hedging management |
| LPs | Remaining share | Return on deposited collateral |

The exact split ratios are configurable per vault and set during vault creation.

## 6. Vault Architecture

### Public Vaults (ERC-4626)

All vaults in Own Protocol are public. There are no private vaults. Each vault is an ERC-4626-compliant tokenized vault where:

- Multiple LPs deposit collateral and receive vault shares proportional to their deposit
- Vault shares represent a claim on the vault's collateral plus accrued spread revenue
- LPs can deposit and withdraw collateral subject to utilization constraints (withdrawals may be restricted when collateral is actively backing minted eTokens)

### Multi-Vault-Manager Model

Each vault supports multiple vault managers. The delegation model works as follows:

1. **LP selects a vault manager**: An LP chooses which vault manager(s) they want to manage the hedging for their share of the vault's exposure
2. **Vault manager accepts**: The vault manager must accept the delegation, creating a mutual agreement
3. **Exposure allocation**: Minted exposure is attributed to specific vault manager(s) based on the collateral they manage
4. **Self-management**: LPs can act as their own vault manager if they prefer to manage hedging directly

This delegation model ensures accountability — vault managers only manage exposure they have explicitly agreed to hedge.

### Vault Lifecycle

1. **Creation**: A vault is created for a specific collateral type. Protocol parameters (minimum collateral ratio, `minSpread`) are set.
2. **LP deposits**: LPs deposit collateral and receive vault shares.
3. **Vault manager assignment**: LPs delegate to vault managers (or self-manage).
4. **Active operation**: Minters mint/redeem against the vault. Spread revenue accrues to the vault.
5. **LP withdrawals**: LPs redeem vault shares for underlying collateral plus accrued yield, subject to utilization limits.
6. **Halt/Resume**: Protocol admin or vault managers can halt a vault in emergency conditions.

### Collateral Ratios

Each vault maintains a collateral ratio — the ratio of total collateral value to total minted notional value:

- **Minimum collateral ratio**: Protocol-enforced floor. If the ratio drops below this threshold, the vault becomes eligible for liquidation.
- **Target collateral ratio**: Recommended operating level, providing a buffer above the minimum.
- **Overcollateralization**: Vaults may hold excess collateral to absorb price movements before requiring hedge adjustments.

## 7. Oracle Architecture

### MVP: Signed Price Feeds

The MVP uses ECDSA-signed price feeds from a protocol-operated oracle signer. Each signed price message includes:

- Asset identifier
- Price (18 decimals)
- Timestamp
- Chain ID
- Verifier contract address

The on-chain `SignedOracleVerifier` contract validates the signature, checks staleness, prevents replay attacks, and returns the verified price.

### Future: Multi-Source Oracles

Post-MVP, the oracle architecture will support:

- M-of-N multi-signer schemes for decentralized price attestation
- Integration with Chainlink, Pyth, and other established oracle networks
- Fallback oracle chains (primary oracle fails, secondary takes over)
- TWAP (time-weighted average price) calculations for additional manipulation resistance

The `IOracleVerifier` interface abstracts the oracle implementation, allowing new oracle types to be added without modifying core protocol contracts.

## 8. eToken Design

### Standard Compliance

Each eToken implements:

- **ERC-20**: Standard fungible token interface
- **ERC-2612 (Permit)**: Gasless approvals via signature, enabling single-transaction mint flows

### Composability

eTokens are designed to be first-class DeFi primitives:

- **Transferable**: eTokens can be freely transferred between addresses
- **Lending collateral**: eTokens can be deposited into lending protocols (Morpho, Aave, Compound) as collateral
- **LP positions**: eTokens can be paired in DEX liquidity pools
- **Yield strategies**: eTokens can be used in yield aggregators and structured products

### Morpho Leverage

eTokens are designed to be compatible with Morpho Blue markets. A user can:

1. Mint eTSLA by depositing USDC
2. Deposit eTSLA as collateral on Morpho
3. Borrow USDC against the eTSLA position
4. Mint more eTSLA with the borrowed USDC
5. Repeat for leveraged exposure

This loop enables leveraged RWA exposure without the protocol itself implementing leverage — it emerges naturally from composability with existing DeFi lending infrastructure.

## 9. Hedging Model

### Offchain Hedging Only

Vault managers maintain offchain hedges in traditional markets to cover the exposure created by minted eTokens. When a minter mints eTSLA, the vault manager purchases the equivalent TSLA exposure offchain (e.g., through a brokerage). When the minter redeems, the vault manager unwinds the hedge.

This approach provides:

- **Real price discovery**: Hedges reference actual market prices, not synthetic oracles
- **Deep liquidity**: Traditional markets offer deeper liquidity than onchain alternatives
- **Regulatory clarity**: Offchain hedging separates the onchain protocol from regulated securities trading

### Vault Manager Obligations

Vault managers are accountable for:

- Maintaining hedges that match their allocated exposure within acceptable tolerances
- Responding to market movements by adjusting hedge positions
- Setting appropriate spreads to compensate for hedging costs and risk
- Ensuring sufficient capital to maintain hedges during volatile periods

The protocol does not verify hedging positions onchain — trust in vault managers is established through reputation, performance history, and the mutual delegation model that allows LPs to choose their preferred managers.

## 10. Revenue Model

### Revenue Sources

All protocol revenue derives from the unified spread:

| Source | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| Minimum spread floor | The `minSpread` parameter guarantees a base level of revenue on every mint/redeem, regardless of vault manager competition |
| Spread revenue share | The protocol receives a fixed share of total spread on every operation |

### Revenue Distribution

```
Spread (applied to mint/redeem)
├── Protocol share (from minSpread floor)
├── Vault manager share (compensation for hedging)
└── LP share (return on collateral)
```

### Economic Sustainability

The `minSpread` floor ensures protocol revenue even in competitive environments where vault managers minimize their spread margins. As protocol volume grows, revenue scales linearly with mint/redeem activity.

## 11. Competitive Position

### vs. Centralized Synthetic Platforms (Synthetix, Mirror)

- **Permissionless**: No governance token staking required to mint. Any collateral holder can participate.
- **Fully collateralized**: No undercollateralized synthetic positions. Every eToken is backed by real collateral in a vault.
- **Composable**: eTokens are standard ERC-20 tokens usable across DeFi, not locked in a protocol-specific ecosystem.

### vs. Tokenized Securities (Securitize, Backed Finance)

- **No KYC at the protocol level**: Permissionless minting and redemption. Compliance can be layered on top by integrators.
- **24/7 availability**: Not restricted to market hours. Spreads adjust, but minting never stops.
- **DeFi-native**: Built for composability with lending, DEXs, and yield protocols.

### vs. Perpetual Futures (GMX, dYdX)

- **Spot-like exposure**: eTokens represent spot-equivalent exposure, not leveraged derivatives. No funding rates, no liquidation of the minter's position.
- **Transferable positions**: eTokens can be moved between wallets, used as collateral elsewhere, or LP'd — perpetual positions cannot.
- **No expiry**: eTokens have no expiration date, unlike futures contracts.

### Unique Advantages

- **Multi-collateral from day one**: USDC, aUSDC, ETH, and stETH supported at launch
- **Yield-bearing collateral**: LPs earn underlying DeFi yield (Aave interest, Lido staking rewards) on top of spread revenue
- **Vault manager marketplace**: Competitive vault managers drive spreads down while maintaining hedging quality
- **ERC-4626 composability**: Vault shares are themselves composable DeFi primitives

## 12. Security Model

### Trust Assumptions

| Actor | Trust Level | Rationale |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| Oracle signer | Trusted (protocol-operated) | Single signer for MVP; M-of-N planned |
| Vault managers | Semi-trusted | Manage offchain hedging; LP delegation model provides accountability and choice |
| Protocol admin | Trusted (multisig) | Asset whitelisting, parameter governance, emergency halt |
| Minters | Untrusted | All input treated as adversarial |
| Liquidation bots | Untrusted | Incentivized by liquidation rewards |

### Vault Manager Delegation Security

The mutual delegation model provides several security properties:

- **Opt-in only**: Vault managers must explicitly accept delegation, preventing unwanted exposure
- **LP choice**: LPs choose their vault managers based on reputation and track record, creating market-driven trust
- **Diversification**: LPs can split their collateral across multiple vault managers to reduce single-manager risk
- **Self-management**: LPs who do not trust any vault manager can manage their own hedging

### Smart Contract Security

- **Checks-Effects-Interactions (CEI)**: All external functions follow CEI pattern
- **ReentrancyGuard**: Applied to every function that transfers tokens
- **Access control**: Explicit modifiers for all privileged operations
- **SafeERC20**: All token transfers use OpenZeppelin's SafeERC20
- **Pinned compiler**: Solidity 0.8.28, no floating pragma
- **Oracle validation**: Signature verification, staleness checks, chain ID binding, replay prevention
- **Formal testing**: Unit, integration, invariant (10,000+ fuzz runs), and fork tests against live deployments

### Liquidation Mechanism

When a vault's collateral ratio falls below the minimum threshold:

1. Any address can call the liquidation function
2. The protocol sells a portion of the vault's collateral on a DEX to restore the ratio
3. The liquidator receives a reward for triggering the liquidation
4. If the vault cannot be restored, it is halted and LPs can withdraw remaining collateral

## 15. Open Questions and Future Considerations

- **Vault manager slashing**: Should vault managers post a bond that can be slashed for hedging failures? This adds accountability but increases capital requirements.
- **Insurance fund**: Should a portion of spread revenue fund an insurance pool for black swan events?
- **Governance token**: Is a governance token needed, or can the protocol be governed by a multisig indefinitely?
- **Cross-margin vaults**: Should vaults support multiple eToken types (e.g., a single vault backs both eTSLA and eGOLD)?
- **Onchain hedge verification**: Can zero-knowledge proofs or attestation services eventually verify offchain hedging positions?
