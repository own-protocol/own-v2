# Own Protocol — Permissionless Tokenized Real-World Assets — Long-Term Product Vision | March 2026

## 1. Overview

Own Protocol enables permissionless, fully collateralized exposure to real-world assets (RWAs) onchain. Users mint composable ERC-20 eTokens (eTSLA, eGOLD, eTLT, etc.) by placing orders in an escrow + claim marketplace. Vault managers compete to fulfill orders by executing offchain hedges. Liquidity providers (LPs) supply collateral to public vaults that act as trustless security for all orders, earning yield through a unified spread model.

The protocol is deployed on Base (Ethereum L2) and built with Foundry.

### Core Thesis

Traditional finance gatekeeps asset exposure behind brokerage accounts, KYC, geographic restrictions, and market-hour limitations. Own Protocol removes these barriers by creating permissionless synthetic exposure backed by real collateral and managed hedging, accessible 24/7 to anyone with a wallet.

## 2. Protocol Participants

### Minters

Any address can mint eTokens by placing an order with supported stablecoins (USDC, USDT, USDS, etc.). The minter's stablecoins go to a vault manager via the escrow marketplace, who executes the offchain hedge. eTokens are minted to the minter upon confirmation. To close a position, minters redeem eTokens and receive stablecoins back, minus the applicable spread.

Minters are treated as untrusted. All minter input is adversarial.

### Liquidity Providers (LPs)

LPs deposit collateral into public vaults and receive ERC-4626 vault shares representing their proportional ownership. LP collateral acts as trustless onchain security guaranteeing that minters' orders are fulfilled. LPs earn yield from spread revenue and, for yield-bearing collateral (aUSDC, stETH), from the underlying asset's native yield.

LPs can also act as their own vault manager, managing their own hedging and spread parameters without delegating to a third party. Self-managing LPs use the same VM interface and are expected to run the same infrastructure.

### Vault Managers

Vault managers actively claim and fulfill orders from the escrow marketplace, execute offchain hedges, and confirm execution onchain. Their responsibilities include:

- Claiming orders from the escrow marketplace and executing offchain hedges
- Maintaining offchain hedges to ensure the vault remains solvent against RWA price movements
- Setting their own spread (must be >= protocol minSpread floor)
- Managing their exposure within self-set caps
- Confirming order execution with signed oracle prices

A vault can have multiple vault managers. LPs delegate to vault managers through a mutual agreement model — the LP selects a vault manager, and the vault manager accepts the delegation. This ensures both parties consent to the arrangement.

Vault managers earn a share of the spread revenue generated by the exposure they manage.

### Protocol Admin

A multisig that governs protocol-level parameters: asset whitelisting, stablecoin whitelisting, oracle configuration, minimum spread floors, maximum utilization caps, and emergency halt capabilities.

### Liquidation Bots

Untrusted external actors that monitor vault health and trigger liquidation when a vault's collateral ratio falls below the required threshold. Liquidation bots are incentivized by a reward paid from the liquidated vault.

## 3. Supported Assets

### eTokens

Each supported RWA has a corresponding eToken — a standard ERC-20 token with ERC-2612 (Permit) support:

| eToken | Underlying | Category |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| eTSLA  | Tesla stock | US Equities |
| eGOLD  | Gold (XAU) | Commodities |
| eTLT   | 20+ Year Treasury Bond ETF | Fixed Income |

eTokens are 18-decimal ERC-20 tokens with admin-updatable name and symbol (to support stock split transitions). They are composable with the broader DeFi ecosystem — holdable, transferable, and usable as collateral in lending protocols.

For dividend-paying assets (eTLT), eTokens include a rewards-per-share accumulator that allows holders to claim their proportional share of dividends.

### Minter Payment (Multi-Stablecoin)

Minters pay in any protocol-whitelisted stablecoin. Each VM sets which stablecoins they accept. Order routing filters by token — only VMs accepting the minter's chosen stablecoin can claim the order.

| Stablecoin | Notes |
|-----------|-------|
| USDC | Primary stablecoin |
| USDT | Widely held |
| USDS | MakerDAO stablecoin |

### LP Collateral Types (Multi-Collateral from Launch)

Own Protocol supports multiple LP collateral types from day one:

| Collateral | Decimals | Yield-Bearing | Handling |
|-----------|----------|---------------|----------|
| USDC | 6 | No | Direct deposit |
| aUSDC (Aave) | 6 | Yes (rebasing) | Yield captured via ERC-4626 wrapper; rebasing balance accrues to the vault |
| ETH | 18 | No | Wrapped to WETH internally via WETHRouter |
| stETH (Lido) | 18 | Yes (exchange rate) | Wrapped to wstETH internally; yield captured via wstETH/stETH exchange rate appreciation |

### Yield-Bearing Collateral Mechanics

**aUSDC**: Aave's interest-bearing USDC is a rebasing token. When deposited as collateral, the vault wraps it in an ERC-4626-compliant adapter. The accruing yield increases the vault's total collateral value, benefiting LPs without any action required.

**stETH**: Lido's liquid staking derivative is internally wrapped to wstETH (wrapped stETH) on deposit. wstETH is a non-rebasing token whose value relative to stETH increases over time as staking rewards accumulate. The vault tracks collateral value using the wstETH/stETH exchange rate, and LPs benefit from the yield.

## 4. Mint and Redeem Lifecycle

### Minting (Order-Based)

1. Minter selects an asset (e.g., TSLA), a stablecoin (e.g., USDC), and order parameters (market with slippage, or limit price)
2. Minter deposits stablecoins into the protocol escrow
3. VMs see the order. An eligible VM claims it (full or partial)
4. Stablecoins are released to the claiming VM
5. VM buys the hedge offchain using the minter's stablecoins
6. VM confirms execution onchain with a signed oracle price
7. Protocol verifies the execution price is within the minter's slippage tolerance
8. eTokens are minted to the minter's address
9. LP collateral in the vault acts as trustless security throughout

### Redeeming (Order-Based)

1. Minter submits eTokens for redemption with price parameters and desired stablecoin for payout
2. eTokens are held in protocol escrow (not burned yet)
3. VM claims the order (must accept the requested stablecoin), unwinds the hedge offchain
4. VM sends stablecoins to the minter and confirms with a signed oracle price
5. Protocol verifies, burns the escrowed eTokens
6. If the VM misses the deadline, LP collateral is sold to pay the minter

### Non-Market Hours

VMs can execute during off-hours (pre/post-market, futures, etc.) at their own spread based on their risk and hedging model. The protocol does not restrict when VMs execute. VMs set per-asset off-hours toggles and separate off-market exposure caps to manage their risk.

## 5. Unified Spread Model

Own Protocol uses a unified spread model. There is no separate "protocol fee" and "vault manager spread" — a single spread is applied to every order.

### VM-Set Spreads with Protocol Floor

Each VM sets their own spread onchain (in BPS), which must be >= the protocol's `minSpread` floor. VMs compete on spread — lower spreads attract more orders from the escrow marketplace.

The `minSpread` is set by the protocol admin and applies uniformly across all vaults.

### Spread vs Slippage

**Spread** is the VM's fee — a known cost of service, visible before the user places an order.

**Slippage** is the user's price movement tolerance — protection against the oracle price changing between order placement and execution. Slippage applies to market orders only; limit orders specify an exact price.

These are cleanly separated: spread is the fee, slippage is the risk tolerance.

### Revenue Split

Spread revenue from each order is split three ways:

| Recipient | Share | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Protocol | Fixed portion of spread (from `minSpread` floor) | Protocol sustainability, development, security audits |
| Vault Manager | Configurable share of spread above floor | Compensation for active hedging management |
| LPs | Remaining share | Return on deposited collateral |

The exact split ratios are configurable per vault and set during vault creation.

## 6. Vault Architecture

### Public Vaults as Security Pools (ERC-4626)

All vaults in Own Protocol are public. There are no private vaults. Each vault is an ERC-4626-compliant tokenized vault that serves as a **trustless security/guarantee pool**:

- Multiple LPs deposit collateral and receive vault shares proportional to their deposit
- Vault shares represent a claim on the vault's collateral plus accrued spread revenue and native yield
- **Vaults are NOT fund-flow intermediaries** — minter stablecoins go to VMs via escrow, not through the vault
- LP collateral is the onchain guarantee that minters' orders will be fulfilled, even if a VM defaults

### Multi-Vault-Manager Model

Each vault supports multiple vault managers. The delegation model works as follows:

1. **LP selects a vault manager**: An LP chooses which vault manager they want to manage the hedging for their share of the vault's exposure
2. **Vault manager accepts**: The vault manager must accept the delegation, creating a mutual agreement
3. **Order claiming**: VMs claim orders from the escrow marketplace. Their claimable exposure is bounded by their delegated LPs' collateral and their self-set exposure caps
4. **Self-management**: LPs can act as their own vault manager if they prefer to manage hedging directly (same interface, same infrastructure)

### Cross-Vault Competition

VMs from different vaults can compete to claim the same order. For example, a VM operating on the USDC vault and a VM operating on the ETH vault can both claim a mint order for eTSLA. The security backing comes from whichever vault the claiming VM belongs to. This creates competition across collateral types, benefiting minters with better pricing.

### Vault Lifecycle

1. **Creation**: A vault is created for a specific collateral type. Protocol parameters (minimum collateral ratio, `maxUtilization`) are set.
2. **LP deposits**: LPs deposit collateral and receive vault shares.
3. **Vault manager assignment**: LPs delegate to vault managers (or self-manage).
4. **Active operation**: VMs claim and fulfill orders. Spread revenue accrues to the vault.
5. **LP withdrawals**: LPs submit async withdrawal requests (ERC-7540 FIFO queue), fulfilled when utilization allows.
6. **Halt/Resume**: Protocol admin or vault managers can halt a vault in emergency conditions.

### Collateral Ratios

Each vault maintains a collateral ratio — the ratio of total LP collateral value to total outstanding eToken exposure:

- **Minimum collateral ratio**: Protocol-enforced floor. If the ratio drops below this threshold, the vault becomes eligible for liquidation.
- **Maximum utilization**: Hard cap. When exceeded, VMs from that vault cannot claim new orders.
- **Overcollateralization**: Vaults may hold excess collateral to absorb price movements before requiring hedge adjustments.

## 7. Oracle Architecture

### MVP: Signed Price Feeds

The MVP uses ECDSA-signed price feeds from a protocol-operated oracle signer. Each signed price message includes:

- Asset identifier
- Price (18 decimals)
- Timestamp
- Sequence number (monotonically increasing)
- Chain ID
- Verifier contract address

The on-chain oracle verifier validates the signature, checks staleness, enforces price deviation bounds, validates the sequence number, and returns the verified price.

Oracle prices are used in two contexts:
1. **Order placement**: to establish the reference price for slippage calculation
2. **Order confirmation**: VM submits a signed oracle price; protocol verifies it's within the user's slippage

### Future: Multi-Source Oracles

Post-MVP, the oracle architecture will support:

- M-of-N multi-signer schemes for decentralized price attestation
- Integration with Chainlink, Pyth, and other established oracle networks
- Fallback oracle chains (primary oracle fails, secondary takes over)
- TWAP (time-weighted average price) calculations for additional manipulation resistance

The `IOracleVerifier` interface abstracts the oracle implementation, allowing new oracle types to be added without modifying core protocol contracts.

## 8. eToken Design

### Standard Compliance

Each eToken implements:

- **ERC-20**: Standard fungible token interface
- **ERC-2612 (Permit)**: Gasless approvals via signature
- **Admin-updatable name/symbol**: Stored as `string storage` with `onlyAdmin` setter, enabling stock split token transitions
- **Rewards-per-share accumulator**: For dividend-paying assets (eTLT), enables continuous dividend distribution

### Composability

eTokens are designed to be first-class DeFi primitives:

- **Transferable**: eTokens can be freely transferred between addresses
- **Lending collateral**: eTokens can be deposited into lending protocols (Morpho, Aave, Compound) as collateral
- **LP positions**: eTokens can be paired in DEX liquidity pools
- **Yield strategies**: eTokens can be used in yield aggregators and structured products

### Morpho Leverage

eTokens are designed to be compatible with Morpho Blue markets. A user can:

1. Mint eTSLA by placing an order with USDC
2. Deposit eTSLA as collateral on Morpho
3. Borrow USDC against the eTSLA position
4. Mint more eTSLA with the borrowed USDC
5. Repeat for leveraged exposure

This loop enables leveraged RWA exposure without the protocol itself implementing leverage — it emerges naturally from composability with existing DeFi lending infrastructure.

### Stock Splits

When a stock split occurs, the old eToken continues at the pre-split price. Admin renames it (e.g., "eTSLA" → "eTSLA-legacy-1"), and a new eToken deploys as "eTSLA" at the post-split price. The asset registry tracks active vs legacy tokens. Old tokens remain valid and redeemable. Migration is organic — no forced migration, no friction for DeFi positions.

### Dividends

For dividend-paying assets (eTLT), eTokens use a rewards-per-share accumulator (Synthetix/MasterChef pattern). VMs receive the actual dividend offchain (they hold the real hedge) and deposit equivalent stablecoins into the reward contract. Any holder can claim accrued dividends at any time. Pending rewards are auto-settled on every transfer.

## 9. Hedging Model

### Offchain Hedging Only

Vault managers maintain offchain hedges in traditional markets to cover the exposure created by minted eTokens. When a VM claims a mint order, they receive the minter's stablecoins and purchase the equivalent RWA exposure offchain. When they claim a redeem order, they unwind the hedge and send stablecoins back to the minter.

This approach provides:

- **Real price discovery**: Hedges reference actual market prices, not synthetic oracles
- **Deep liquidity**: Traditional markets offer deeper liquidity than onchain alternatives
- **Regulatory clarity**: Offchain hedging separates the onchain protocol from regulated securities trading
- **Direct capital flow**: The minter's stablecoins fund the hedge directly — no intermediate steps

### Vault Manager Obligations

Vault managers are accountable for:

- Claiming and fulfilling orders within deadline constraints
- Maintaining hedges that match their allocated exposure within acceptable tolerances
- Responding to market movements by adjusting hedge positions
- Setting appropriate spreads to compensate for hedging costs and risk
- Confirming execution with valid signed oracle prices

The protocol does not verify hedging positions onchain — trust in vault managers is established through reputation, performance history, and the mutual delegation model that allows LPs to choose their preferred managers.

## 10. Revenue Model

### Revenue Sources

All protocol revenue derives from the unified spread:

| Source | Mechanism |
|--------|-----------|
| Minimum spread floor | The `minSpread` parameter guarantees a base level of revenue on every order, regardless of VM competition |
| Spread revenue share | The protocol receives a fixed share of total spread on every operation |

### Revenue Distribution

```
Spread (applied to every order)
├── Protocol share (from minSpread floor)
├── Vault manager share (compensation for hedging)
└── LP share (return on collateral)
```

### Economic Sustainability

The `minSpread` floor ensures protocol revenue even in competitive environments where vault managers minimize their spread margins. As protocol volume grows, revenue scales linearly with order activity.

High utilization naturally increases LP yield (same revenue, less collateral), attracting new LP deposits and creating organic equilibrium.

## 11. Competitive Position

### vs. Centralized Synthetic Platforms (Synthetix, Mirror)

- **Permissionless**: No governance token staking required to mint. Anyone with stablecoins can participate.
- **Fully collateralized**: No undercollateralized synthetic positions. Every eToken is backed by real LP collateral in a vault.
- **Composable**: eTokens are standard ERC-20 tokens usable across DeFi, not locked in a protocol-specific ecosystem.
- **Order marketplace**: Open competition among VMs drives execution quality and pricing.

### vs. Tokenized Securities (Securitize, Backed Finance)

- **Permissionless**: No KYC at the protocol level. Compliance can be layered on top by integrators.
- **24/7 availability**: Not restricted to market hours. VMs set their own off-hours spreads.
- **DeFi-native**: Built for composability with lending, DEXs, and yield protocols.

### vs. Perpetual Futures (GMX, dYdX)

- **Spot-like exposure**: eTokens represent spot-equivalent exposure, not leveraged derivatives. No funding rates, no liquidation of the minter's position.
- **Transferable positions**: eTokens can be moved between wallets, used as collateral elsewhere, or LP'd — perpetual positions cannot.
- **No expiry**: eTokens have no expiration date, unlike futures contracts.
- **Dividends**: eTLT holders receive dividend pass-through — perpetual futures do not.

### Unique Advantages

- **Multi-collateral from day one**: USDC, aUSDC, ETH, and stETH LP collateral at launch
- **Multi-stablecoin payments**: Minters can pay in any whitelisted stablecoin
- **Yield-bearing collateral**: LPs earn underlying DeFi yield (Aave interest, Lido staking rewards) on top of spread revenue
- **Vault manager marketplace**: Open escrow + claim marketplace drives competitive pricing
- **Cross-vault competition**: VMs from different vault types compete for the same orders
- **ERC-4626 composability**: Vault shares are themselves composable DeFi primitives
- **Flash-loan resistant by design**: Stablecoins exit to VMs (can't be recovered in same tx), eTokens are burned on redeem

## 12. Security Model

### Trust Assumptions

| Actor | Trust Level | Rationale |
|-------|-------------|-----------|
| Oracle signer | Trusted (protocol-operated) | Single signer for MVP; M-of-N planned |
| Vault managers | Semi-trusted | Claim orders, execute hedges offchain, confirm with oracle price; LP delegation model provides accountability; LP collateral backstops VM default |
| Protocol admin | Trusted (multisig) | Asset whitelisting, stablecoin whitelisting, parameter governance, emergency halt |
| Minters | Untrusted | All input treated as adversarial |
| Liquidation bots | Untrusted | Incentivized by liquidation rewards |

### Vault Manager Delegation Security

The mutual delegation model provides several security properties:

- **Opt-in only**: Vault managers must explicitly accept delegation, preventing unwanted exposure
- **LP choice**: LPs choose their vault managers based on reputation and track record, creating market-driven trust
- **Diversification**: LPs can split their collateral across multiple vault managers to reduce single-manager risk
- **Self-management**: LPs who do not trust any vault manager can manage their own hedging
- **Collateral backstop**: If a VM defaults, LP collateral is liquidated to make the minter whole — LPs have skin in the game

### Smart Contract Security

- **Checks-Effects-Interactions (CEI)**: All external functions follow CEI pattern
- **ReentrancyGuard**: Applied to every function that transfers tokens
- **Access control**: Explicit modifiers for all privileged operations
- **SafeERC20**: All token transfers use OpenZeppelin's SafeERC20
- **Pinned compiler**: Solidity 0.8.28, no floating pragma
- **Oracle validation**: Signature verification, staleness checks, price deviation bounds, sequence numbers, chain ID binding
- **Flash-loan resistance**: Architectural — stablecoins exit to VM, eTokens are burned
- **Formal testing**: Unit, integration, invariant (10,000+ fuzz runs), and fork tests against live deployments

### Liquidation Mechanism

When a vault's collateral ratio falls below the liquidation threshold:

1. **Tier 1**: Liquidator provides eTokens → vault burns them, sends discounted LP collateral
2. **Tier 2**: New LP deposits at depressed share price → organic recovery
3. **Tier 3**: Vault sells LP collateral on DEX (fallback, also for redemption deadline expiry)
4. If the vault cannot be restored, it is halted and enters wind-down

## 13. Roadmap

### Phase 1 — MVP (Current)

- Order-based minting/redemption with escrow + claim marketplace
- Multi-stablecoin support for minters (USDC, USDT, USDS)
- Multi-collateral LP vaults: USDC, aUSDC, ETH (via WETH), stETH (via wstETH)
- Public vault architecture with ERC-4626 shares as security pools
- Vault manager delegation model (multi-manager per vault)
- Signed oracle price feeds (single signer) with deviation checks and sequence numbers
- Unified spread model with `minSpread` floor — VMs compete on spread
- Three-tier liquidation engine
- Async LP withdrawal queue (ERC-7540 pattern)
- Dividend pass-through via rewards-per-share accumulator
- Stock split handling via soft migration
- Initial asset set: eTSLA, eGOLD, eTLT
- Offchain hedging by vault managers

### Phase 2 — UX Optimization and Decentralization

- **Signed intents** for atomic single-transaction execution (EIP-712 intent + Permit2)
- M-of-N oracle signer scheme for decentralized price attestation
- Integration with Chainlink and Pyth oracle networks
- Expanded asset coverage: additional equities, commodities, forex pairs, and fixed income
- Vault manager reputation and performance tracking system
- Insurance fund from protocol spread revenue as first-loss tranche
- Governance framework for protocol parameter updates

### Phase 3 — Advanced DeFi Integration

- Morpho Blue market deployment for eToken leverage
- eToken yield strategies and structured products
- Multi-asset vaults (ERC-7575) for diversified collateral pools
- Native ETH vault support (ERC-7535) via WETHRouter
- Integration with major DEX aggregators for improved liquidation execution
- Cross-chain deployment (Ethereum mainnet, Arbitrum, Optimism)

### Phase 4 — Protocol Maturity

- Fully decentralized oracle network
- ZK attestation of offchain hedge positions
- On-chain vault manager performance verification
- Protocol-owned liquidity strategies
- Institutional-grade APIs and compliance tooling for integrators
- Community governance transition
- Expansion to non-EVM chains

## 15. Open Questions and Future Considerations

- **Vault manager slashing**: Should vault managers post a bond that can be slashed for hedging failures? This adds accountability but increases capital requirements.
- **Insurance fund sizing**: What portion of protocol spread revenue should fund the insurance pool? How large does it need to be to meaningfully cover tail risk?
- **Governance token**: Is a governance token needed, or can the protocol be governed by a multisig indefinitely?
- **Cross-margin vaults**: Should vaults support multiple eToken types (e.g., a single vault backs both eTSLA and eGOLD)?
- **Onchain hedge verification**: Can zero-knowledge proofs or attestation services eventually verify offchain hedging positions?
- **Intent solver network**: Should Phase 2 intents be routed through a solver network (like CoW Protocol) for optimal execution?
- **DeFi dividend wrappers**: Should the protocol provide wrapper contracts that auto-claim dividends for eTokens held in Uniswap pools or lending protocols?
